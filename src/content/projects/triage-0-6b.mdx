description: "Fine-tuned a 0.6B model to turn raw support emails into structured ticket JSON, lifting priority accuracy from 38% to 87% and running fully offline."
---

## Problem

Support teams triage inbound tickets by hand. Someone reads a raw email, judges its priority, and pulls out the fields that route it. It is slow, it drifts from person to person, and it does not scale. A stock small model does not fix it. Base Qwen3-0.6B got ticket priority right only 38% of the time on held-out data. That is close to unusable for anything feeding a routing queue. The goal was a model small enough to run offline on a consumer GPU that still turns a raw email into structured ticket JSON, priority included, accurately enough to act on.

## Approach

I fine-tuned Qwen3-0.6B to take a raw support email as input and emit structured ticket JSON with a priority field. Two techniques did the work. LoRA fine-tuning adapted the small model without retraining most of its weights. Knowledge distillation from a larger DeepSeek teacher let the 0.6B student learn to copy the teacher's triage labeling instead of learning from sparse labels alone.

The main design decision was to keep the whole thing small and local. The fine-tuned model is exported to GGUF and served through LM Studio on a Radeon RX 6750 XT over Vulkan. It runs fully offline with no inference-time API cost. A 0.6B model is the constraint that makes the project interesting. It forces the accuracy to come from the training, not from scale.

## Verification

The main check was held-out accuracy on the field that matters most for routing, priority. On 1,200 tickets held out of training, priority accuracy rose from 38% with the base model to 87% after fine-tuning. The base number is not decoration. It is the control. Running the same 1,200 tickets through the untuned model and the fine-tuned model, with identical grading, shows the lift comes from the training rather than from the task being easy.

Priority is a discrete label, so grading is exact match against the ground-truth priority, not a soft similarity score that can be talked up. A prediction counts only when the emitted priority equals the true priority. Because the 1,200 tickets were separated before any fine-tuning, the 87% reflects generalization to unseen emails rather than memorization of the training set.

Distillation gives the evaluation a second anchor. The 0.6B student is trained to reproduce the larger DeepSeek teacher's labeling on the triage task, so the target is a concrete reference rather than a vague notion of quality. And because the model runs fully offline in LM Studio with no inference-time API, evaluation runs against a fixed local artifact rather than a remote model that can change underneath you. The held-out number reflects one stable system, which is what makes a figure like 87% worth quoting.

## What broke

The honest hazard is the output format, not the accuracy number. A 0.6B model is tiny, and a model that small emitting JSON can produce malformed or structurally invalid output that a downstream parser rejects. That is separate from whether the priority label inside it is correct. I have not measured that invalid-structure rate here, so I will not claim it is solved.
